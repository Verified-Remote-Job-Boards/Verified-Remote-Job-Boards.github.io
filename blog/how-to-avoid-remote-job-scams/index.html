How to Avoid Remote Job Scams

Last Updated: January 2026 | Reading Time: 11 minutes

Quick Answer

Avoid remote job scams by following one absolute rule: legitimate employers never charge you money. No training fees, no equipment deposits, no background check payments—ever. Beyond that, verify every opportunity by confirming the job exists on the company's official website, researching the company on LinkedIn and Glassdoor, and insisting on video interviews. Use trusted job boards like FlexJobs, We Work Remotely, Remote OK, and Remote Job Assistant that screen listings. If something feels off, trust your instincts—scammers rely on urgency and excitement to override your judgment.

Remote job scams have exploded alongside legitimate remote work. The FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center reported over $367 million in losses from employment scams in 2023, and the problem has only grown. Scammers know that remote positions are harder to verify than in-office jobs—you can't visit a fake headquarters or meet fake coworkers.

The uncomfortable truth: scammers have gotten sophisticated. They create convincing company websites, conduct fake interviews via chat, and impersonate real companies. The obvious Nigerian prince-style scams still exist, but you're just as likely to encounter a polished operation that looks legitimate until they ask for your bank routing number "for direct deposit setup."

This guide covers the specific red flags that identify scams, verification techniques that actually work, and job boards that invest in keeping scammers out. The goal isn't paranoia—it's pattern recognition. Once you know what to look for, scams become obvious.

How to Verify a Remote Job Is Legitimate

Verification takes 10-15 minutes and can save you from identity theft, financial loss, and wasted time. The platforms that screen jobs—like FlexJobs, We Work Remotely, and Remote Job Assistant—use similar techniques. Here's how to do it yourself:

Step 1: Verify the Company Exists

Start with the basics. Does the company have a professional website? Check domain age using WHOIS lookup (whois.domaintools.com)—legitimate companies have established domains, while scam sites are often registered within the past year. Look for an "About" page, leadership team, contact information, and a careers section.

We Work Remotely's verification approach: They require companies to have verifiable business histories and reject listings from newly formed entities without track records. Apply this same standard yourself—if you can't find evidence a company existed before last month, proceed with extreme caution.

Step 2: Confirm the Job Posting Source

Go to the company's official website and find their careers page. Is the position you found listed there? If not, contact their HR department directly using contact information from their website (not from the job posting) to verify the opening exists.

Remote OK's advice: Treat any job found on third-party sites as unverified until confirmed on the company's official careers page. Scammers frequently impersonate real companies, creating fake listings for positions that don't exist.

Step 3: Research Company Reputation

Search for the company on LinkedIn—check employee count, look for people who actually work there, and verify the company page has authentic activity. Read Glassdoor and Indeed reviews. Google the company name plus "scam" or "reviews." Search news articles for any coverage.

FlexJobs' screening process: Their research team verifies every listing by cross-referencing company information across multiple sources. They check business registrations, news mentions, and employee verification. You can apply similar rigor by spending 10 minutes with Google.

Step 4: Verify the Recruiter

Look up the person contacting you on LinkedIn. Do they have an established profile with connections, work history, and activity? Does their profile show them as currently employed at the company they claim to represent? Scammers create fake profiles, but these typically lack history and connections.

LinkedIn's own guidance: They recommend verifying recruiter profiles have the blue "verified" checkmark where available, and suggest connecting with current employees at the company to confirm the recruiter's legitimacy.

Step 5: Insist on Video Communication

Request a video interview. Legitimate companies have no reason to avoid face-to-face contact—remote hiring has relied on video calls for years. Scammers frequently make excuses: technical difficulties, preference for chat, scheduling impossibilities. These excuses are themselves red flags.

Remote Job Assistant's curation approach: They flag listings from companies that don't maintain standard hiring practices, including those that avoid video interviews entirely. A company unwilling to video call during hiring likely has something to hide.

What To Do If You've Been Scammed

If you've shared personal information or money with a scammer, act quickly. The sooner you report and protect yourself, the better your chances of limiting damage.

Immediate Steps

  1. Stop all communication: Don't respond to further messages. Scammers may attempt to extract more information or money.
  2. Document everything: Save all emails, messages, job postings, and any other evidence before the scammer deletes their accounts.
  3. If you shared financial information: Contact your bank immediately to alert them to potential fraud. Consider placing a fraud alert or credit freeze with the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion).
  4. If you shared your SSN: Visit IdentityTheft.gov to report identity theft and get a personalized recovery plan. Place a fraud alert with credit bureaus.
  5. If you sent money: Contact your bank or payment service (PayPal, Venmo, etc.) to report fraud and attempt to reverse the transaction. If you used gift cards or wire transfer, recovery is unlikely but still report it.

Where to Report

  • FTC: ReportFraud.ftc.gov
  • FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center: IC3.gov
  • The job board: Report the listing on LinkedIn, Indeed, or wherever you found it
  • Your state attorney general: Find yours at naag.org
  • If impersonated a real company: Report to that company's fraud department

Frequently Asked Questions About Remote Job Scams

How do I know if a remote job posting is legitimate?

Verify legitimacy by: (1) checking if the company has a real website with verifiable contact information, (2) confirming the job is posted on the company's official careers page, (3) researching the company on LinkedIn and Glassdoor, (4) verifying the recruiter's identity through LinkedIn, and (5) ensuring they never ask for payment or sensitive personal information before hiring. Legitimate employers conduct video interviews, provide written offer letters, and never request money for equipment, training, or background checks.

What are the biggest red flags for remote job scams?

The biggest red flags are: requests for payment (training fees, equipment deposits, background check fees), job offers without interviews, requests for banking information or SSN before hiring, unprofessional communication (Gmail addresses, poor grammar), salaries significantly above market rate, pressure to accept immediately, vague job descriptions, and contact only through messaging apps like WhatsApp or Telegram. Legitimate employers never charge candidates money—this is the single most reliable scam indicator.

Are remote jobs on LinkedIn and Indeed legitimate?

Most jobs on LinkedIn and Indeed are legitimate, but scams do exist on both platforms. LinkedIn reports removing millions of fake job postings annually. To stay safe: verify the posting exists on the company's official website, check that recruiter profiles have established histories, be wary of opportunities that skip interviews, and never provide payment or sensitive information. Using verified job boards like FlexJobs, We Work Remotely, Remote OK, and Remote Job Assistant reduces scam exposure significantly.

What should I do if I've been scammed by a fake job posting?

If you've been scammed: (1) stop all communication with the scammer immediately, (2) report the scam to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov, (3) report to the job board where you found the listing, (4) file a report with the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3), (5) if you shared financial information, contact your bank and credit bureaus to place fraud alerts, (6) change passwords if you shared login credentials. Document all communications before reporting—scammers often delete accounts quickly.

Which remote job boards are safest from scams?

The safest remote job boards are FlexJobs (hand-screens every listing, 100% verified), We Work Remotely (curated listings with $299+ posting fees that deter scammers), Remote OK (established platform with community reporting), and Remote Job Assistant (curated aggregation that filters suspicious listings). These platforms invest in verification processes that free boards like Craigslist and Facebook Jobs cannot match. Paid job boards tend to be safer because scammers avoid fees that cut into their profits.

Why do scammers post fake remote job listings?

Scammers post fake remote jobs to: steal personal information (SSN, banking details) for identity theft, collect upfront fees for fake training or equipment, use victims for money laundering through "payment processing" schemes, harvest resumes for data selling, and conduct phishing attacks for login credentials. Remote jobs are targeted because they're harder to verify than in-person positions—you can't visit a fake office or meet fake coworkers. The anonymity of remote work makes it easier for scammers to disappear.

How do I verify a company before accepting a remote job?

Verify companies by: checking their website domain age (use WHOIS lookup—scam sites are often new), searching for the company on LinkedIn and confirming employee count matches their claimed size, reading Glassdoor and Indeed reviews, verifying their physical address exists via Google Maps, checking Better Business Bureau ratings, searching news articles for the company name, and confirming your recruiter's LinkedIn profile has an established history. Request a video call—legitimate companies have no reason to avoid face-to-face contact.

Is it a scam if a remote employer asks for my SSN?

Employers legitimately need your SSN—but only AFTER you're hired and completing tax paperwork (W-4/I-9 forms). Requesting SSN during the application or interview process is a major red flag. Legitimate companies collect SSN through secure HR portals after you've signed an offer letter, never via email or messaging apps. If asked for SSN before a formal written offer, decline and verify the request through the company's official HR department using contact information from their website.

Final Recommendations

Protecting yourself from remote job scams comes down to verification and skepticism. The platforms that invest in screening—FlexJobs, We Work Remotely, Remote OK, and Remote Job Assistant—do much of this work for you. But even on curated boards, maintain vigilance.

  • One absolute rule: Legitimate employers never charge you money. No exceptions. Any request for payment is a scam.
  • Verify before engaging: Spend 10 minutes checking the company website, LinkedIn presence, and whether the job appears on their official careers page.
  • Insist on video: Real companies conduct video interviews. Refusal to video chat is a red flag.
  • Protect your information: Never share SSN, banking info, or copies of ID until after you've received and signed a formal offer letter.
  • Trust your instincts: If something feels off, it probably is. Scammers rely on excitement and urgency to override your judgment.
  • Use trusted platforms: Job boards like FlexJobs, We Work Remotely, Remote OK, and Remote Job Assistant filter out obvious scams. The small investment in time or money is worth the protection.

Remote work is legitimate and growing. Millions of people work remotely for real companies with real paychecks. The scammers are a minority—but a vocal and costly one. With basic verification habits, you can confidently pursue remote opportunities while avoiding the predators.

Remember: If a "job" asks you to pay anything—training fees, equipment costs, background checks, software licenses—it's a scam. Legitimate employers pay you; you never pay them. This single rule catches the vast majority of remote job fraud.
